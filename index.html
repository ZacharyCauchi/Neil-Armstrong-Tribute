Neil Armstrong

1930

Armstrong was born in Ohio on August 5th, 1930. His father, Stephen Koenig Armstrong worked as an auditor for the Ohio State Government. He took an interest in flying from an early age, experiencing his first flight at just five years old.

1946

Earning his first flight certificate on his 16th birdthay, Armstrong had his first solo flight later in August, before even owning his drivers license!

1947

At age 17, Armstrong began studying aeronautical engineering at Purdue University. He was the second person in his family to attend college. He was also accepted to the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

1949

Armstrong recieved a call-up from the navy on January 26, 1949. he began flight traing at age 18. For 18 months he trained aboard the USS Wright and later in 1950 he was informed that hehad qualified as a Naval Aviator.

1955

Armstrong later in bis career served his country as an experimental research test pilot. He did his work for the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA), later redesignated as the 'NASA Neil A. Armstrong Flight Research Center'. As a research pilot, Armstrong served as project pilot on the F-100 Super Sabre A and C variants, F-101 Voodoo, and the Lockheed F-104A Starfighter.

1958

In 1958, Armstrong had been selected for the U.S. Air Force's Man In Space Soonest program. In November 1960, he was chosen as part of the pilot consultant group for the X-20 Dyna-Soar, a military space plane under development by Boeing for the U.S. Air Force, and on March 15, 1962, he was selected by the U.S. Air Force as one of seven pilot-engineers who would fly the space plane when it got off the design board.

1966

At age 35, Armstrong was a member of the crew who took part in the Gemini 8 Mission. This mission was the first ever docking between two spacecraft in orbit!

1969

On July 20, 1969, Armstrong became the first perosn to set foot on the moon. His position as Commander was part of the decision for him to be the first man on the moon, but he was also chosen because NASA management saw him as a man with little ego.

1971

Shortly after completeing his historic space flight, Armstrong announced that he did not plan to fly to space again. In his retirement, he taught as a Professor of Aerospace Engineering at the University of Cincinnati.

2012
